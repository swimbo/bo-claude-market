## Lesson 1

**Purpose**: Present the first and often most surprising or attention-grabbing lesson
**Target length**: ~100 words

State the lesson as a clear, declarative sentence. Then provide brief context — what happened that taught you this? Include a specific detail or moment that makes it real. End with why this lesson matters beyond your personal experience.

## Lesson 2

**Purpose**: Build on the first lesson with a complementary or contrasting insight
**Target length**: ~100 words

Follow the same structure: state the lesson, share the story behind it, and connect it to the reader's world. Vary your storytelling approach from Lesson 1 — if the first was a personal anecdote, make this one an observation or a data point.

## Lesson 3

**Purpose**: Deliver the middle lesson — often the most nuanced or counterintuitive one
**Target length**: ~100 words

This is a good place for your most surprising or counterintuitive lesson. Challenge an assumption the reader likely holds. Provide enough evidence or reasoning to make the counterintuitive point credible. Keep it punchy and memorable.

## Lesson 4

**Purpose**: Provide a practical, immediately actionable lesson
**Target length**: ~100 words

Make this lesson the most tactical and applicable. Give the reader something they can do today based on what you learned. Be specific about the action and the expected result. Practical lessons are the most shared and bookmarked.

## Lesson 5

**Purpose**: Close with the deepest or most philosophical lesson
**Target length**: ~100 words

End on a resonant, reflective note. This lesson should feel like wisdom rather than tactics — something that shifted your perspective or changed how you approach your work or life. Let it linger.

## Key Insight

**Purpose**: Tie all five lessons together into a single overarching takeaway
**Target length**: ~100 words

Step back and identify the thread connecting all five lessons. What is the meta-lesson? State it clearly and explain why it matters. This is your gift to the reader — the one thing they should remember if they forget everything else. Close with a question or invitation that prompts reflection.
