## Problem Statement

**Purpose**: Clearly articulate the problem and its visible impact
**Target length**: ~250 words

Define the problem in specific, measurable terms. Avoid vague descriptions — use data, examples, and real consequences. Describe what the problem looks like from the outside: what symptoms are people experiencing? Who is affected and how severely? Quantify the impact where possible (revenue lost, time wasted, customers churned). Establish urgency by framing what will happen if the problem continues unaddressed. Make the reader feel the weight of the problem before moving to diagnosis.

## Root Cause Analysis

**Purpose**: Dig beneath the surface to identify the true driver of the problem
**Target length**: ~300 words

Move beyond symptoms to identify the underlying cause. Use a structured approach — the "5 Whys," fishbone diagram, or systems thinking — to trace the problem to its origin. Show your reasoning: why is this the root cause and not one of the more obvious surface-level explanations? Address common misconceptions about what is causing the problem and explain why they are incomplete or incorrect. Support your analysis with evidence, patterns, or data that point to the true cause. This section should create an "aha" moment where the reader sees the problem in a new light.

## Proposed Solution

**Purpose**: Present a solution that directly addresses the root cause
**Target length**: ~250 words

Describe the solution with enough specificity that the reader can evaluate its viability. Explain how it directly targets the root cause identified above, not just the symptoms. Address feasibility: What resources, time, and commitment does this solution require? Acknowledge trade-offs and potential objections. If there are alternative solutions, briefly explain why this one is preferred. Include evidence or precedent from similar situations where this approach has worked.

## Next Steps

**Purpose**: Provide a concrete action plan for moving forward
**Target length**: ~200 words

Break the path forward into 3-5 specific, time-bound action items. For each step, identify who is responsible and what the expected outcome is. Prioritize by impact and sequence — what must happen first? Include milestones or checkpoints for evaluating progress. End with a clear first action the reader can take immediately, lowering the barrier to getting started.
