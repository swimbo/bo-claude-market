## The Context

**Purpose**: Set the stage for the mistakes by describing the situation and your intentions
**Target length**: ~100 words

Briefly describe the activity, project, or endeavor where these mistakes occurred. What were you trying to accomplish? What was your experience level at the time? This context helps the reader calibrate — were you a beginner making beginner mistakes, or an experienced person who should have known better? Both are valid and relatable. Establish that your intentions were good even though the execution was flawed.

## Mistake 1

**Purpose**: Describe the first mistake candidly with its consequences
**Target length**: ~150 words

State the mistake clearly in one sentence. Then explain: What did you do (or fail to do)? What happened as a result? How did you realize it was a mistake — immediately, or only in hindsight? Include specific details that make the mistake concrete and believable. End with the lesson this specific mistake taught you. Be honest about whether you would have listened if someone had warned you in advance.

## Mistake 2

**Purpose**: Describe a different type of mistake for variety and breadth
**Target length**: ~150 words

Follow the same structure as Mistake 1 but choose a different category of mistake. If the first was about execution, make this one about strategy or mindset. Vary the tone slightly — perhaps this mistake was more embarrassing, more costly, or more subtle. Include the consequences and the lesson. Show the reader that mistakes come in many forms and that awareness is the first defense.

## Mistake 3

**Purpose**: Deliver the most impactful or surprising mistake
**Target length**: ~150 words

Save your most significant or unexpected mistake for last. This could be the one that cost the most, took the longest to recognize, or challenged your deepest assumptions. The third mistake should feel like a crescendo. Include the consequences and the lesson. If this mistake is still teaching you something, say so — not all lessons are fully learned.

## The Biggest Lesson

**Purpose**: Identify the meta-lesson that connects all three mistakes
**Target length**: ~150 words

Step back and identify what all three mistakes have in common. What underlying pattern, blind spot, or assumption connected them? State the overarching lesson clearly. This is the insight that elevates the piece from a list of errors to genuine wisdom. The biggest lesson is often about humility, awareness, or the gap between theory and practice.

## How You Can Avoid These

**Purpose**: Translate your painful experience into actionable guidance
**Target length**: ~150 words

Provide specific, practical advice for someone embarking on the same endeavor. What would you do differently if you started over? What systems, habits, or questions could prevent these mistakes? Be realistic — some mistakes may be unavoidable learning experiences. For those, advise on how to minimize damage and recover faster. Close with encouragement: making mistakes is not the problem; refusing to learn from them is.
