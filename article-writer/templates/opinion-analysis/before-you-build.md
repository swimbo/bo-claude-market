## The Common Mistake

**Purpose**: Describe the premature action that so many people take
**Target length**: ~150 words

Identify the specific action people rush into without adequate preparation. Use a scenario the reader will recognize — perhaps they are about to do this very thing. Explain why this mistake is so common: Is it driven by excitement, social pressure, fear of missing out, or misguided advice? Be empathetic, not condescending — you may have made this same mistake yourself. State what they are about to build, launch, invest in, or commit to, and signal that there is a critical step they are skipping.

## Why This Is Risky

**Purpose**: Make the stakes concrete so the reader takes the warning seriously
**Target length**: ~200 words

Quantify the risk where possible — wasted time, lost money, damaged reputation, missed opportunity cost. Use a real example or case study of someone who made this mistake and suffered the consequences. Explain the cascading effects: how this one premature decision creates downstream problems that are harder and more expensive to fix later. Address the psychological trap that keeps people from heeding this warning — "it won't happen to me" thinking, sunk cost fallacy, or optimism bias. Make the reader genuinely pause and reconsider.

## The Better Path

**Purpose**: Offer a validated alternative approach
**Target length**: ~200 words

Present the recommended approach as a clear, positive alternative — not just "don't do X" but "do Y instead." Explain the logic behind this better path: Why does it work? Who has used it successfully? What makes it more reliable than the common approach? Include enough specificity that the reader can envision following this path. Address the most likely objection: "But that takes longer / costs more / feels less exciting." Explain why the short-term cost is worth the long-term benefit.

## Your Action Plan

**Purpose**: Give the reader concrete next steps they can start today
**Target length**: ~150 words

Provide 3-5 specific, time-bound action steps. Each step should be small enough to complete in a single sitting. Order them sequentially so the reader knows exactly what to do first. For each step, briefly note the expected outcome so the reader knows they are on track. Close with encouragement and a reminder of what they are protecting by taking the better path. The reader should finish this section feeling prepared and motivated, not scolded.
