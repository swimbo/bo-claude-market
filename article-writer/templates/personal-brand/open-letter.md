## Why I'm Writing

**Purpose**: Establish the motivation for the letter and your right to speak
**Target length**: ~250 words

Address the recipient directly. Explain what compelled you to write this letter publicly rather than privately. What moment, event, pattern, or realization triggered it? Establish your connection to the topic and your standing to speak on it — not to gatekeep, but to give context for why your perspective matters. Be clear about your intentions: Is this a plea, a challenge, a thank-you, an apology, or a call to arms? Set the emotional tone early. The opening should make the reader understand that this letter had to be written.

## My Message

**Purpose**: Deliver the core of what you need to say with clarity and emotion
**Target length**: ~400 words

This is the heart of the letter. Speak directly and honestly. Organize your message around 2-3 key points, but let them flow naturally rather than forcing a rigid structure — letters are personal, not presentations.

For each point:
- State what you want the recipient to understand or acknowledge.
- Support it with a specific story, observation, or piece of evidence.
- Explain the impact — on you, on others, or on the broader community.

Use the second person ("you") to maintain the direct address throughout. Balance emotion with reason — passion makes the letter compelling, but logic makes it persuasive. If you are expressing frustration or criticism, be firm without being cruel. If you are expressing gratitude or admiration, be specific about what moved you. The best open letters feel like the writer is speaking difficult truths with love.

## What I Ask of You

**Purpose**: Close with a specific, actionable request or challenge
**Target length**: ~250 words

Transition from message to ask. Be specific about what you want the recipient to do, change, consider, or commit to. Frame the ask in terms of its impact: what will be different if the recipient responds? If the ask is large, break it into smaller, more achievable steps. Acknowledge the difficulty of what you are asking — change is hard, and recognizing that builds empathy.

Close the letter with a statement that looks forward. Express hope, determination, or solidarity. Leave space for the recipient to respond with dignity. Sign off with your name and any relevant affiliation. The closing should feel like an extended hand, not a closed fist.
