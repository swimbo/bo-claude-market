## The Advice I Wish I Had

**Purpose**: Share the most important lessons in a direct, personal voice
**Target length**: ~350 words

Write directly to your younger self using second person ("you"). Be specific about the advice — not "work harder" but "stop saying yes to every project that comes your way." Cover 3-5 pieces of advice, each grounded in a specific situation or mistake you can reference. For each piece of advice:

- State it clearly and directly.
- Briefly reference the experience that taught it to you (without fully telling the story — hint at it).
- Explain what would have been different if you had known this earlier.

Keep the tone warm and honest, not bitter or regretful. Write as someone who has made peace with the journey, not someone who resents it. The advice should feel like it comes from a wise older sibling or mentor, not a critic.

## Why This Matters

**Purpose**: Connect the advice to deeper truths about growth and resilience
**Target length**: ~250 words

Step back from the specific advice to reflect on the bigger picture. Why were these lessons so hard to learn in real time? What made you resistant to hearing this kind of advice when you were younger? Explore the gap between knowing something intellectually and truly understanding it through experience. This section adds depth — it is not just about what you would say, but about why wisdom requires time and experience to develop. Connect these reflections to the reader, who is likely at their own stage of learning.

## Keep Going

**Purpose**: Close with encouragement and reassurance
**Target length**: ~200 words

End the letter with warmth and reassurance. Tell your younger self what turns out well — without spoiling the journey. Acknowledge the hard parts that are coming but express confidence in your ability to handle them. Reassure your younger self about the worries that consume them now but turn out not to matter. Close with a message that applies equally to your younger self and to the reader: that the path forward is rarely clear, but it is always worth walking. Sign off as you would sign a real letter.
