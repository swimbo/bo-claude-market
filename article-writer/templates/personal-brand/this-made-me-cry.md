## The Moment

**Purpose**: Describe the trigger event vividly so the reader can experience it too
**Target length**: ~150 words

Set the scene. Where were you? What were you doing? Then describe what happened — the video you watched, the conversation you overheard, the news you read, the kindness you witnessed. Be specific enough that the reader can picture it. Include the sensory details that made the moment hit: a tone of voice, a facial expression, a single sentence that landed differently than expected. Do not explain why it affected you yet — just let the moment stand on its own. The goal is to let the reader feel something before you analyze anything.

## My Response

**Purpose**: Share your raw emotional reaction with authenticity
**Target length**: ~150 words

Describe what happened inside you. Did your eyes well up? Did you laugh until you could not breathe? Did your jaw clench with anger? Be honest about the intensity of your response. If the emotion surprised you, say so. If you tried to suppress it, admit that. This section works because emotional honesty is rare in public writing. Do not perform the emotion or exaggerate it — understatement is often more powerful than hyperbole. Let the reader see a real human responding to something real.

## What It Reveals

**Purpose**: Explore what this emotional reaction tells us about something deeper
**Target length**: ~150 words

Move from emotion to meaning. Why did this moment hit so hard? What does your reaction reveal about your values, fears, hopes, or unresolved experiences? Connect the personal to the universal — if this moved you, it likely touches something shared. Explore the deeper theme: Is this about justice, connection, loss, generosity, or something else entirely? This is where the piece becomes more than a personal anecdote — it becomes commentary on the human experience.

## The Lesson

**Purpose**: Distill the experience into a takeaway the reader can carry forward
**Target length**: ~150 words

Close with what this experience taught you or reminded you of. State the lesson simply. Invite the reader to pay attention to their own strong emotional reactions — they are often signals pointing toward what matters most. If appropriate, include a gentle call-to-action: watch the video, read the article, have the conversation, pay attention to the overlooked. End on a note that honors the original emotion without over-explaining it.
