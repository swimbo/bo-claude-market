## The Wound

**Purpose**: Describe the painful experience with honesty and vulnerability
**Target length**: ~250 words

Tell the story of what happened without softening it. What was the failure, loss, rejection, or setback? Be specific about the circumstances: when it happened, what led to it, and what the immediate consequences were. Resist the urge to foreshadow the lesson — let the reader sit in the discomfort of the experience. Include enough context that the reader understands why this hurt so much. The wound should feel real, not curated for social media. If you played a role in causing it, own that honestly.

## Emotional Low Point

**Purpose**: Get real about the emotional impact without performing vulnerability
**Target length**: ~200 words

Describe how the wound felt internally. What emotions dominated — shame, anger, grief, fear, confusion? How did it affect your confidence, relationships, or daily functioning? This section should be raw but not performative. The distinction matters: raw means honest and unpolished; performative means exaggerating pain for sympathy. Share the thoughts you had that you would normally keep private. Describe the low point — the moment when things felt worst. This is where the reader who has experienced their own wounds will feel deeply seen.

## The Turning Point

**Purpose**: Identify the moment or process that began the shift from pain to growth
**Target length**: ~200 words

What changed? Was it a conversation, a book, a realization, a passage of time, or a deliberate choice? Describe the turning point with specificity. It rarely arrives as a dramatic epiphany — more often it is a slow shift in perspective. Explain what allowed you to see the experience differently. Who or what helped? Be honest about how long the turning took. Give credit where credit is due — to mentors, friends, therapists, or simply to time. The turning point should feel organic, not forced.

## How It Applies Today

**Purpose**: Connect the hard-won wisdom to current life and the reader's experience
**Target length**: ~250 words

Show how the lesson from this wound shapes your decisions, relationships, or work today. Provide specific examples of how you now act differently because of what you learned. Then extend the wisdom to the reader: How can they apply this without needing to experience the same wound? Offer practical guidance, not platitudes. Acknowledge that some wounds never fully heal, and that is acceptable — wisdom does not require closure. Close with a message that honors both the pain and the growth, without implying that suffering is necessary or desirable.
