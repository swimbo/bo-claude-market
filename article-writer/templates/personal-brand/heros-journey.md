## Ordinary World

**Purpose**: Establish the starting point so the reader understands what was at stake
**Target length**: ~200 words

Describe life before the journey began. What was normal? What were your assumptions, routines, and comfort zones? Paint a picture that the reader can relate to — many people are living in their own ordinary world right now. Include specific details that make the scene real: your job, your mindset, your daily life. Hint at the underlying dissatisfaction or gap that you may not have recognized at the time but that made you ripe for change.

## Call to Adventure

**Purpose**: Describe the trigger event that disrupted the status quo
**Target length**: ~200 words

What happened that changed everything? This could be a crisis, an opportunity, a conversation, a failure, or a flash of insight. Describe the moment with specificity — when and where it happened, who was involved, what was said or done. Capture the emotional response: excitement, fear, confusion, resistance. Be honest about any reluctance or denial. Most heroes initially resist the call before accepting it. Show the reader the moment of decision: the point where you chose to step into the unknown rather than retreat to safety.

## Struggle and Trials

**Purpose**: Walk through the difficult middle where growth actually happens
**Target length**: ~300 words

This is the heart of the story and where most people gloss over too quickly. Describe the real struggles: the failures, the doubt, the moments you wanted to quit. Include 2-3 specific trials with enough detail that the reader can feel the difficulty. What did you try that did not work? Who helped you, and who let you down? What resources or inner strength did you discover along the way? Be vulnerable — the reader connects with your humanity in this section, not your success. Show the gradual progression from despair to capability, even if it was not linear.

## Transformation

**Purpose**: Reveal the pivotal change — the moment of breakthrough or realization
**Target length**: ~300 words

Describe the turning point. What shifted in your understanding, ability, or perspective? This might be a single dramatic moment or a gradual dawning. Explain not just what changed but how it changed you — your thinking, your priorities, your identity. Compare who you were before and after. Use concrete evidence of the transformation: different decisions you made, different results you achieved, different responses you had to familiar situations. The transformation should feel earned — a direct result of the struggles described above, not a lucky break.

## Return with the Lesson

**Purpose**: Share the wisdom gained and how it applies to the reader's journey
**Target length**: ~200 words

In the hero's journey, the hero returns home with a gift for the community. What is yours? State the lesson clearly and connect it to the reader's own life. How can they apply this lesson without having to go through the same struggle? Be generous with your wisdom but honest about its limits. Acknowledge that every journey is different. Close with encouragement for readers who are in their own ordinary world, facing their own call to adventure, or struggling through their own trials. The best hero's journey stories make the reader feel like the hero.
