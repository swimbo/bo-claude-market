## Then (Before)

**Purpose**: Honestly describe your past belief and why you held it
**Target length**: ~200 words

Describe what you used to believe, how you used to operate, or the mindset you previously held. Be specific and fair — do not caricature your past self. Explain why this belief made sense at the time given your knowledge, experience, and context. Include a concrete example of a decision or action that reflected this old belief. The reader should be able to relate, because many of them likely hold this same belief right now. Treat your past self with compassion, not contempt.

## Catalyst

**Purpose**: Describe what triggered the shift in your thinking
**Target length**: ~150 words

What happened that made you question the old belief? This might be a single dramatic event, a gradual accumulation of evidence, a mentor's challenge, or a failure that forced reconsideration. Describe the catalyst with enough detail that the reader understands its impact. Include the emotional response — confusion, resistance, curiosity, or excitement. The best catalysts create cognitive dissonance: the moment when what you believed and what you experienced could no longer coexist.

## Now (After)

**Purpose**: Articulate your evolved belief with clarity and conviction
**Target length**: ~200 words

State your current perspective clearly. Explain how it differs from the old belief and why you consider it an improvement. Provide evidence — results, outcomes, or experiences — that validate the new approach. Be nuanced: acknowledge what you lost in the transition, not just what you gained. If the new belief is still evolving, say so. Intellectual honesty about uncertainty is more credible than false certainty. Include a concrete example of a recent decision or action that reflects your evolved thinking.

## Why It Matters

**Purpose**: Connect your personal evolution to the reader's growth potential
**Target length**: ~150 words

Explain why this shift matters beyond your personal story. What does it reveal about growth, adaptation, or the nature of expertise? Speak directly to readers who may be where you were. Resist being preachy — share, do not lecture. Offer a question or prompt that helps the reader identify their own "then vs. now" moments. Close with a message that normalizes changing your mind as a sign of strength, not weakness.
