# Section Structure — Detailed Reference

Guidance for writing each standard whitepaper section.

## 1. Cover Page

Include:

* Title: clear, value-oriented, indicates what the reader will learn. Not clever or cryptic.

* Subtitle (optional): adds specificity to the title

* Author name(s) and credentials

* Organization name and logo

* Publication date

* Version number (for documents that will be revised)

The title is the single most important element for whether someone reads the paper. Frame it around reader value:

* Poor: "Introducing Acme's New Cloud Platform"

* Good: "Reducing Cloud Infrastructure Costs by 40%: A Technical Evaluation"

## 2. Abstract

Use for technical/academic audiences. 150-250 words.

Rules:

* Written in introduction-body-conclusion paragraph form

* No images, tables, or graphics

* No subsections or bullet points

* Covers: what the paper is about, the approach taken, the key finding

* Written so the reader can decide whether to read the full document

## 3. Executive Summary

Use for business/decision-maker audiences. Maximum 1 full page.

Rules:

* Can include high-level graphics

* Must be a standalone document in miniature

* Written for readers who may not read the full paper

* Covers: problem, purpose, results, conclusions, and recommendations

* Understandable regardless of technical knowledge

When to use Abstract vs. Executive Summary:

* Abstract: technical/academic papers, journal submissions, peer-reviewed publications

* Executive Summary: business papers, product evaluations, anything where decision-makers are the primary audience

* Both: only for very long documents (25+ pages) with both technical and executive audiences

## 4. Table of Contents

Required for papers exceeding 6 pages.

Rules:

* Clickable/linked sections in digital formats

* Hierarchical numbering (1.0, 1.1, 1.2, 2.0) aids navigation

* Include page numbers

* Update after all editing is complete (last step before finalization)

## 5. Introduction / Problem Statement

This section must accomplish four things:

1. Hook the reader with the problem's urgency and scale
2. Use data to establish severity ("87% of organizations face X")
3. Provide industry context for why this matters NOW
4. State the thesis or purpose of the paper clearly

Rules:

* Do NOT present the solution here. Only define the problem.

* Include a "roadmap paragraph" that tells readers what to expect in the rest of the document

* Keep to 1-2 pages maximum

* Open with a compelling statistic, question, or scenario

## 6. Background / Context

This section aligns the reader so the solution will make sense.

Include:

* Historical context and foundational concepts

* Relevant prior work and existing approaches

* Key term definitions and acronym explanations

* Statistics and research findings that frame the landscape

* Why existing approaches are insufficient

Calibrate depth to audience:

* Expert readers: brief background, focus on what is new

* Mixed audiences: thorough background with definitions

* Newcomer audiences: educational, ground-up explanation

## 7. Methodology

Include only for research-based papers.

Must cover:

* How data was collected (surveys, experiments, interviews)

* Sample size and selection criteria

* What tools, models, or frameworks were used

* How findings were validated

* Limitations of the approach

This section is what separates original research from opinion. Without it, a paper's claims are assertions, not findings.

## 8. Body / Analysis / Solution

The core substance. This is where the paper delivers its value.

Organization:

* Use clear headings and subheadings (H2, H3)

* Break into logical subsections

* Include case studies, real-world examples, or benchmarks

* For product-oriented papers: explain HOW the solution works without becoming a sales pitch

* For technical papers: include architecture diagrams, code snippets, comparison tables

Rules:

* Quantify results ("reduced latency by 43%", not "dramatically faster")

* Support every claim with evidence

* Use callout boxes for key statistics or expert quotes

* Break dense text with bullet points and visuals

* Each subsection should be 500-800 words maximum

## 9. Results / Findings

Can be a separate section or merged with the Body.

Include:

* Measurable outcomes with specific numbers

* Before/after comparisons

* Performance benchmarks with test conditions

* Peer-reviewed validation (if available)

* Data visualizations (charts, graphs, tables)

## 10. Conclusion

Rules:

* Summarize the problem and solution arc

* Restate key findings — do NOT introduce new information

* Provide concrete recommendations or next steps

* Frame for action: what should the reader do now?

* Keep to 1 page maximum

* Do not repeat the introduction verbatim — synthesize

## 11. Call to Action

Include only for commercially-oriented papers.

Rules:

* Appears at the very end, after establishing credibility

* Suggests logical next steps: demo, consultation, deeper resource, contact information

* Must feel earned, not forced

* Readers who reached the CTA have read the entire paper — reward them with a clear, easy path forward

* One primary CTA only. Do not overwhelm with options.

## 12. References / Bibliography

Rules:

* Cite ALL sources used in the document

* Format per the citation style appropriate to the field

* Cross-check that every in-text citation has a reference entry and vice versa

* Verify all URLs are accessible

* Sort according to citation style convention (IEEE: numerical order; APA: alphabetical)

## 13. Appendices / Glossary

Include when supplementary material would interrupt narrative flow.

Appropriate content:

* Full datasets and raw data tables

* Extended charts and detailed figures

* Mathematical formulas and proofs

* Process diagrams and system configurations

* Code samples and API references

* Full glossary of acronyms and technical terms

Rules:

* Label as Appendix A, Appendix B, etc.

* Reference in-text ("see Appendix A")

* Include a brief description at the start of each appendix
